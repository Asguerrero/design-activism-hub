FROM SMALL BEGINNINGS TO CURRENT TRENDS

1971

Although teachings in design activism remain nascent, their most essential ideas were first inspired by the design historian Victor Papaneka. In 1971, Papapaneka published Design For Real The World, where he outlined the need for a new design theory. He encouraged creative professionals to have a more proactive role in improving human livelihood and sustainable development.

1997

Through his book, The Struggle for Utopia, design historian Victor Margolin examined how modern artists informed turning connections between social ideals and art. Margolin introduces innovative ideas about how visual communicators became more and more essential to contemporary politics.

2004

In the past decades, various exhibitions have had further discussions about designing for social good. In 2004, designer Bruce Mau and the Institute without Boundaries organized Massive Change, a multimedia exhibition about the role of design in addressing economic and political systems.

2007

Similarly, in 2007, Cooper Hewitt National Design Museum displayed Design for The Other 90%, exploring thirty design-driven projects that developed low-cost solutions to decrease global socioeconomic disparity.

2018

Arturo Escobar published Designing The Pluriverse (2018), which introduced "a new vision of design theory and practiced aimed at channeling design's world-making capacity toward ways of being and doing that are deeply attuned to justice and the Earth."

2019

Elizabeth Resnick published The Social Design Reader. This publication compiles critical texts from the last 50 years that encompass and emphasize socially responsible design. It documents the development of social design.

2020

Most recently, Sasha Costanza-Chock published Design Justice. It is “An exploration of how marginalized communities might lead the design, dismantle structural inequality, and advance collective liberation and ecological survival.”
